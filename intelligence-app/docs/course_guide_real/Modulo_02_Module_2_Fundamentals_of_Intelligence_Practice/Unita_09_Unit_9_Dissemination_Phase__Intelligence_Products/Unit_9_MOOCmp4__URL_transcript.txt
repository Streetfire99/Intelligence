Let's now look inside the product itself. What are the typical components of a well-structured intelligence report? While formats may vary, there are some core elements that appear again and again. First, the executive summary, ideally written in BLOOF style. This is where the key judgment is presented up front. Then, a section on context or background. This helps the reader understand why this issue matters and what triggered the analysis. Next, the core findings, is the analytical heart of the product. It includes evidence, interpretation, reasoning and scenario considerations, if needed. After that, we often include implications or recommendations. What does it mean for the organization? What actions should be considered? Finally, depending on the complexity, we may add footnotes, source evaluations, confidence levels or annexes. These structures help build transparency, logical flow and usability. It allows the reader to quickly understand, trust and apply what they're reading. One of the most important and probably most difficult questions in intelligence work is how certain are you? You may have a solid judgment, you may believe in your conclusions, but if you don't communicate your level of confidence, you're not giving your reader the full picture. Confidence level helps the user understand how strong the evidence is, how consistent the sources are and how many assumptions were required. In many organizations, we use terms like low, moderate or high confidence. Sometimes we also use probabilistic language, likely, unlikely, very likely. These labels are not decorations, they are analytical tools. They allow readers to assess risk, to compare assessment and to act with the appropriate level of caution. Expressing uncertainty is not a weakness, it is a sign of professionalism, because no one expects perfect foresight, but they do expect honesty about the limits of the knowledge, of course. Intelligence products are not encyclopedias, they are not supposed to just describe that what is known, they must also offer a professional opinion. Because we analysts work with facts, we rely on data, but at the end of the day, intelligence is about interpretation. It's about telling the reader, here is what we think is happening, here is what we expect and here's why. Of course, that opinion must be grounded. It must be supported by reasoning, evidence and methodology. We are not in the business of speculation, we are not in the business of assessment. So don't be afraid to express a judgment, but always explain how you reached it. So remember, decision makers want clarity, they want insight, they don't want you sit on the fence, they want to know what you think and how confident you are about it.
